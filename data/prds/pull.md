## Problem
The illustrated pull-back-car teardown was a hit because everyone owned one and nobody knew a coiled spring + flywheel was doing the work. That intuition — drag back, store energy, release — is a physics lesson people would *feel* if you let them play with the real numbers instead of a cartoon.

## What it is
A single-screen toy in the drag-a-physical-property mold. Drag the car backward: a spring compresses with real Hooke's-law force, an energy meter fills (½kx²). Release: energy dumps into a flywheel, then to the wheels, then across a floor whose friction coefficient you pick (ice, wood, carpet, sandpaper). The car travels the honest distance. Push past the spring's limit and torque flips it — the built-in viral clip. Enrichment ramp: swap spring constants, wheel mass, add a ramp, race two cars.

## v1 (humiliatingly small)
One car, one flat floor, one spring. Drag left to charge (show the energy number), release, car decelerates to a stop at the physically-correct distance. No friction menu, no flip, no art beyond a rectangle. Just: drag further → goes further, and the number is real.

## Done means
You can drag the block back, feel the spring resist, release, and it coasts to a stop — and pulling twice as far sends it roughly four times as far, because ½kx² is actually driving it.
