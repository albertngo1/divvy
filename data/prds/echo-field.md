## Problem
Every survival game on the Steam charts (Raft, Sons of the Forest, Don't Starve) identifies materials by sight — you *see* wood and you *know* it's wood. The mmWave material-classification radar on HN suggests a fresher core loop: what if identification itself were the gameplay, and your eyes lied?

## What it is
A foggy/low-light survival roguelite where materials look ambiguous but each has a distinct radar return (metal rings bright, water absorbs, organic scatters, plastic ghosts). You sweep objects with a scanner and learn to read waveforms: a plant that *looks* edible but echoes like the poison one is a trap. Progression is your growing personal 'signature library' — the game teaches real dielectric intuition sideways. Tension comes from scanning costing battery/time while threats close in.

## v1 (humiliatingly small)
One room, three objects that all render as identical grey blobs. Point the scanner at one → show a simple 1-D echo trace (a line with a characteristic peak per material). Player types/guesses the material; correct guess reveals it. No survival, no threats, no map — just 'objects look the same, the echo tells you which is which.'

## Done means
A player who's never seen the game can, after two or three scans, correctly distinguish 'metal' from 'water' from 'plant' purely by the shape of the echo trace — the read-the-signal skill clicks.
