## Overview
Flick is a browser party game (3–8 players, one shared screen or phones) that turns real anti-cheat forensics into a spot-the-imposter round. It's the weird bridge between three feed items: awesome-game-security (aimbot detection heuristics), osu! (raw input as gameplay), and PostHog-style session replay. Each round you're shown several anonymized input replays; one belongs to a bot. Find it.

## Problem
Social-deduction games are saturated, but almost none give players a real forensic skill to sharpen. Meanwhile the actual tells anti-cheat teams use — inhumanly smooth aim, snap-to-target with zero overshoot, suspiciously constant keypress intervals — are visual, learnable, and genuinely fun to hunt once you know them.

## How it works
Each round, N players are each shown a short (~5s) replay: a moving dot to "track" and targets to "hit," rendered as a fading trail of their real mouse path plus a keypress rhythm strip. The catch: one replay per round is machine-generated to mimic play, injected with a subtle cheat signature (perfect tracking, zero-overshoot flicks, or metronomic key timing). Everyone privately votes which replay is the bot. Correct votes score; the bot's "owner" (or the game, if bot-controlled) scores if it survives. Over rounds the bot's tells get subtler, teaching real detection intuition. Post-game reveal overlays the smoking-gun metric (e.g. "overshoot variance: 0.0px").

## Technical approach
Stack: plain TypeScript + Canvas, no backend needed for local play (WebRTC or a tiny Node relay for phones). Capture real players' `pointermove`/`keydown` timestamps into a trace `{t, x, y}`. Bot traces are generated by a spline through targets with tunable jitter, overshoot, and reaction-time noise — the difficulty knob literally dials human-ness up. Detection-teaching metrics computed live: overshoot distribution, inter-key interval coefficient of variation, aim-path curvature entropy — the same features cheap heuristic anti-cheats use. The hard part is generating bot traces that sit convincingly near the human boundary without being trivially perfect or obviously robotic.

## v1 scope
- Hot-seat local play, one shared screen, pass-the-device voting
- One minigame (track-and-flick), one cheat signature (zero-overshoot flicks)
- Canvas replay renderer with fading trail + keypress strip
- Post-round reveal of the one distinguishing metric

## Out of scope
- Online matchmaking, accounts, leaderboards
- Multiple cheat archetypes and difficulty ramp
- Anti-cheat as an actual product

## Risks & unknowns
- Bot traces may be too easy or impossible to spot — needs playtest tuning
- Short replays might not carry enough signal to be fair
- Fun ceiling: is watching input replays engaging past a few rounds?

## Done means
Four people in a room can play a 5-round hot-seat match where each round shows real captured input traces plus one injected bot, players vote, and across rounds the average correct-guess rate lands between 40–70% (evidence the bot sits at a genuinely tricky human boundary).
