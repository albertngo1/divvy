## Overview
Outgas is a self-hosted ambient daemon that turns a homelab's telemetry into a single evolving image: your server as a small rocky world whose atmosphere is escaping to space. It's for homelabbers who already have Grafana but want something that *feels* like the slow health of their machine rather than a chart.

## Problem
The HN item about helium escaping a nearby rocky exoplanet's atmosphere is quietly poignant — a planet in the habitable zone losing what keeps it alive. Server health is the same story told in dashboards nobody stares at. Disks fill, SMART reallocated-sector counts creep up, pools degrade — all real, all slow, all invisible until failure. There's no artifact that lets you *feel* the year of slow loss.

## How it works
A nightly job samples metrics and maps them onto planetary physics: free storage → atmospheric density (a thicker, hazier limb when healthy); SMART pending/reallocated sectors and pool errors → escape rate (a brighter, longer comet-like tail streaming off the sunward side); uptime → rotation; temperature → the color of the glow. Each night renders one frame and appends it to two artifacts: a live desktop/TV wallpaper showing today's planet, and a horizontal "tree-ring" strip where each column is one night — a full year becomes a printable poster of your atmosphere thinning. A degrading drive literally makes the tail grow over weeks.

## Technical approach
Stack: a small Go or Python daemon reading `smartctl --json`, `zpool status`, `df`, and `node_exporter`/`/proc`. Rendering is a single GLSL fragment shader (headless via `moderngl` or a WASM canvas) — a raymarched sphere with a Fresnel atmosphere term and a particle/streak pass for the escaping tail, seeded deterministically from the day's metric vector so the same inputs always reproduce the same frame. Data model: an append-only SQLite table of `{date, free_bytes, smart_score, temp, err_count}`; the poster is generated by replaying the table. Key detail: normalize each metric against its own rolling baseline so the art responds to *change*, not absolute values, and so a fresh 40TB pool and a full 1TB SSD both read as "healthy" when stable. Hardest part is making the tail legible at both one-frame and 365-column scales without tuning per machine.

## v1 scope
- Read free-space + one SMART health metric from one host
- Nightly render of a single PNG planet frame
- Append-only year strip PNG
- Runs as a systemd timer / launchd job

## Out of scope
- Multi-host constellations
- Real-time animation (it's a nightly still)
- Alerting (Grafana already does that)

## Risks & unknowns
Mapping metrics to "escape" without it looking arbitrary is a taste problem; too subtle and nothing seems to happen for months. Needs a `--simulate` mode that fast-forwards a fake failing drive so you can see the tail grow without waiting.

## Done means
After 30 real nights it produces a 30-column strip where a manually degraded test drive (inject fake SMART errors) is visibly a longer, brighter tail than a healthy one, and today's frame renders as a desktop wallpaper on macOS and a Linux TV.
