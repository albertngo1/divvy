## Overview
A browser party game in the Spaceteam mold. Each player joins a room on their own phone and gets a private control panel of absurdly labeled knobs, sliders, and switches. Commands stream in at a rising tempo — "SET FLURP TO 7", "ENGAGE THE PICKLE VALVE" — but the target control is on someone else's phone. The only interface between phones is your voice. You shout, you fumble, the ship shakes, everybody laughs.

## Problem
Spaceteam is one of the most reliably fun party games ever made, but it lives on phones you have to pass around or install a native app for. Nothing in the browser captures that specific shouting-strangers-are-now-friends chaos. And most co-op web games make private information optional — here, private information is the entire premise.

## How it works
4–6 players join with a room code. Each phone shows a control panel of 6–10 randomly labeled controls drawn from an absurd noun pool. Every 2–4 seconds a command appears on one random phone: "SET FLURP TO 3." That player's phone almost never owns the FLURP knob — someone else does. So you yell. Meanwhile your own queue is stacking up. Miss too many commands and the ship's hull integrity ticks down; hit a target hull damage and the round ends. Score is seconds survived.

## Technical approach
Socket.IO on Albert's homelab, served over Tailscale for testing, later PartyKit/Durable Objects for public rooms. Server owns the authoritative state: control roster per phone, active commands, hull integrity, tempo curve. Clients render a mobile-first React panel — tap knobs to set values, drag sliders, flip switches. Commands are generated by a template engine (verb + weird-noun + value) with a small curated list; no LLM in v1. Label pool is a static JSON of ~120 nouns. Voice is entirely IRL — no WebRTC, no mic access. The per-phone architecture is load-bearing: if commands and controls were on one shared screen, you'd just silently point. Splitting the interface across phones forces spoken coordination.

## v1 scope
- 4-player fixed count
- One 90-second round
- 8 controls per phone from a pool of ~40 labels
- Command tempo ramps from 4s to 1.5s
- Hull integrity bar; game ends at 0 or timer expiry
- No accounts, no persistence, no scoreboard beyond final seconds-survived

## Out of scope
- Speech recognition (voice is IRL only)
- Variable player counts
- Multiple rounds or escalating difficulty tiers
- Custom label packs
- Reconnect/mid-game join

## Risks & unknowns
- Command generation feels repetitive after 2 rounds without richer templates
- Latency spikes on Tailscale kill the tempo illusion
- Groups of 3 or 5 feel bad if 4 is hardcoded
- Some labels are unpronounceable — playtesting will surface the duds

## Done means
Four people in a room with four phones can join, survive or lose a 90-second round of shouted knob-twisting, and want to play a second round without being asked.
